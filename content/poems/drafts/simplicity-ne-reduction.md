Lately, I've been thinking a lot about simplicity.
What does being simple means? How does it look like? Why is it important?
I ruminate about this as I meditate, do the groceries, or weed the garden.

As a fortunate person with the possibility to develop the skill to read and speak a second language, I learned its definition by the hundreds of times I'd looked it up on my text books, so I thought I had a clear comprehension of what it implied.

However, when I began to pay closer attention to the way I talked, and an interesting pattern arose. Even though I would not say it directly, the meaning I was imbuing whenever I used the word simplicity, was all about reduction.

The description for reduction and simplicity may seem similar, but not quite. If you go to a thesaurus, you won't even get those terms as similar words, (at least not in the [online version of the Merriam webster](https://www.merriam-webster.com/thesaurus/simplify)) and yet, I had been using them interchangeably.

I let the intellectualization of its meaning get in the way of its true embodiment.

For me, simplifying is the act of taking something complex and breaking it down into its components to make it easier to grasp, usually by eliminating what is not needed. So, what is the problem with that understanding? That it's incomplete. And while I am in no position to change Merriam Webster definitions (nor it is necessary!), there is an almost imperceptible and pervasive premise behind this view.

Simplifying an intricate, rich, multifaceted situation and strip it of its fullness by "trying to downscale or eliminate" some of its most sublime qualities that allows it to exist in the first place, creates an illusion of transforming and then seeing that situation almost as something trivial. Something you understood and don't need to give it much thought anymore, an inconsequential resolved matter that you can wave away as a nagging mosquito.

It is worth highlighting the damage this view of a person, problem or habit could do to our own relationships, as it stops us from acting accordingly. Take the climate change for example. There are many of us who have reduce the situation to a point of not understanding the impact of making even small change in our daily life, leaving it all to great corporations or governments.

However simplicity is not that. 
Simplicity entails going deep into the matter until fully submerged. And rather than having a comfortable, direct "view" that allows us to dismiss the question, it is the aspiration of going to the core to understand profoundly, it is not 2-2 = 0, but adding 2+2 = 4 together to give birth to a new perspective, that integrates all in one.
Whenever there is a crisis in my life, I have seen my mind go back and forth between the urge to reduce and the need to simplify. Due to my preference of avoiding anguish, tension, conflict or emotional turmoil, reduction often seems extremely appealing. Here doubt, lack of confidence, distrust, nihilism, negativity, superficiality and comparison are the most effective tools to convince myself I do not need to resolve that conflict, that I can canned it and ship it to the space irrelevant things have on my brain. You 
see? Simple. If this conflict or question is making someone uncomfortable? Ding! Gone. Simple. Until you make it an art and you are convinced comfort is the guide to peace.
However Its effects are similar to a painkiller, rather than a cure and the struggle returns full force after some good 8 hours. 

In contrast, whenever I do try to be simple what it is required is completely different. Kindness, compassion, pause, inquiry, presence, acknowledgment, openness and curiosity are needed to embrace the pain, distress and uncomfortable feelings thoughts and emotions this opportunity brings. The task of collecting or putting together all the little pieces of our soul is not an easy task with a clear pathway.

It requires exploration, willingness and the strength to embrace tenderly the exiled emotions we have left behind to fend for themselves. Being simple means taking in, every paradox until there is only one truth to be held. No choosing, nor denying what seems convenient.
Holding the paradox in a way, we don't tilt to one side or another, but to stay firmly balanced in reality.

What I was missing was the invitation or I may say even guidance this 
crisis is presenting me. The integration of yet another fragment of my 
psyche.

And that practice requires courage. That requires seeing clearly how that came to be, until we can see out the complete pattern that keeps repeating. The off and on switches but not get installed and the lights that signalize other action to become. Cause the truth is, we are as automatized as a swiss clock giving the hour so precisely, running smoothly, so accurate, without a fault. All our clogs are perfectly greased by thoughts that trigger continually, making us go round and round 24-7.

Simplicity is the art of de-automating ourselves. 
Taking apart all the machinery to understand deeply all the pieces and redefine what has been. It is not about treating it as scrap and leave it on the deshuesadero. It's not the abandonment or exile of pieces we think doesn't work anymore. Unfortunately, current materialism teaches us we can always buy a new washing machine or computer, without having to think about the destiny of our previous electrodomestic. 
But our hearts, life and experiences are delicate treasures not replaceable
But our hearts are delicate part of experience are not replaceable or disposable parts. They have the wisdom to discover freedom, and are our most precious belongings 

What I was missing was the invitation or I may say even guidance this 
crisis is presenting me. The integration of yet another fragment of my 
psyche.

Whenever we take the invitation to integrate, they are at our disposal, available.
So please, don't make the same error I've been doing for such a long time and embrace yourself dearly, take the chance to go deep in this path of transformation, this murky, messy space, where most of us fear to go, but deep transcendence awaits, transcendence lies beyond.
As the poet Rabindranath Tagore said: "It's very simple to be happy, but it is very difficult to be simple." Our task is simple but not easy, as Joseph Goldstein asks us to be mindful of over and over again. 

Until later,
Diana.
